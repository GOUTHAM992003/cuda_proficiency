# 1.2 Why Do We Need More Speed or Parallelism?

If you write a program that uses **data parallelism** (doing the exact same math operation on lots of data all at once), running that code on a GPU gives huge benefits.

* With just a few hours of work, you can easily get a **10x speedup**.
* If you write the code really well, you can get over a **100x speedup** compared to running it on a single CPU core.

But this brings up a fair question: **Why do we even need that much speed?**
For normal daily use, our phones and laptops open apps instantly and run perfectly fine. Aren't computers fast enough already?

The answer is: Yes, they are fast enough for *today's* basic apps. But the future belongs to **"superapplications,"** and these need a crazy amount of computing power.

Here is a look at the areas that really need more speed and parallelism:

---

## 1. Molecular Biology & Medicine

Currently, biologists rely heavily on microscopes (optics and electronics) to observe the world at a molecular level. However, physical instruments have hard limits on what they can see.

To get around these limits, scientists use **computer simulations** to model how molecules act. With simulations, scientists can test ideas and see details that are impossible to see with a real microscope.

* **Why it needs speed:** The faster our computers are, the larger the biological systems we can simulate, and the longer the reaction times we can model without waiting months for the computer to finish. This massive parallel speed directly leads to medical breakthroughs and life-saving drugs.

## 2. Next-Generation Video and Audio

We all remember the huge jump in quality from old box TVs to modern HD screens. But video technology is still evolving.

* **Why it needs speed:** The future of TV and video involves 3D imaging and crazy AI processing—like taking a low-quality video and having the TV automatically upscale it to high-res on the fly. To do things like adjusting lighting, focus, and depth in real-time, future TVs will need massive parallel computing power built right into them.

## 3. Advanced User Interfaces (UI)

The way we interact with computers is always changing. We went from clunky keyboards to smooth touchscreens on phones.

* **Why it needs speed:** The next generation of interfaces will mix the virtual world with the physical world (like Augmented Reality). Devices will rely heavily on **Computer Vision** and voice processing to understand what you are looking at and saying in real-time. Processing a live camera feed of the real world requires a ton of parallel speed.

## 4. Realistic Gaming and "Digital Twins"

Think about racing games from the 90s. If your car hit a wall, the game just played a canned animation and dropped your score. The car didn't actually "break."

* **Why it needs speed:** Modern games use **real-time physics**. If you crash a car now, the game's physics engine calculates exactly how the metal bends and how the wheels snap.

This hyper-realistic physics led to something called **"Digital Twins"** in the engineering world. A digital twin is a perfect, accurate computer model of a real object (like a bridge or a jet engine). Engineers can run stress tests on the digital twin to guess exactly when and how it will break in real life, which saves millions of dollars. Calculating accurate real-world physics requires massive amounts of parallel math.
